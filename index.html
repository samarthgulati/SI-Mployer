SI Mployer

Helping you understand the job situation after UMSI


Below we have an interactive visualization displaying data from 2011–2014 career surveys. Through the use of multiple selections, we will let you explore the data and identify tradeoffs between different jobs, locations, and salaries.

There are a couple of things to keep in mind while looking through the data. First of all, keep in mind that the data shown below only indicates salary. Additional benefits like insurance, vacation or sick days, retirement, and other fringe benefits are not reflected. Also keep in mind that salary is not adjusted based on inflation or on cost of living.

The Visualizations

The first visualiziation is the salary display. This diplays the salary of all the jobs from our dataset, organized by state. Color corresponds to Job Category. By using the slider below the scatterplot, you can select a specific salary range for filtering.

Below the scatterplot are three tabs. These tabs allow filtering by location, company, and job category. The Location tab shows the US state map. The color saturation indicates the index of cost of living in each state (as of 2015). We can see that New York and California have the highest cost of living. Keep cost of living in mind when looking at salary -- one location with high salaries may also have very high housing or other costs.

The second tab shows us a cluster map of all the companies from the data. These companies have been organized by company sector. The sectors indicate how the company gets funded -- is it funded by the university, the government, a public entity, etc. The size of the clusters show us how many people from our data are working in each sector or company. For example, we can see at a glance that a large number of students from UMSI are currently working in the private or university sector.

The third tab shows a tree map of all the job title organized by job category. These categories are the same color as the ones in the scatterplot. The size of the treemap section indicates how many people have that job title. For example, we can see at a glance that there are a signifiant number of people working as User Experience or UX Designers in the data.
